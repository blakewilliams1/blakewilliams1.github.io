Head

I started with a 3d printed head-base that I bought from an established maker named MixedCandy, off of their etsy page they sold a base with the expression "grumpy K9". Onto this I added several layers of masking tape in alternating grain direction and drew on the pattern where color boundaries would be, along with more annotations to help with cutting and keeping track of how the pieces align after they're off the head. We'll use these pieces as a pattern to cut fur later.

I had a personal goal of designing the interior to be completely removable everywhere that my head physically touched for easier cleaning. I used quarter-sized disk magnets on the inside of the base, paired with matching magnets inside of spandex covered foam "cups" that wrap around the sides and back of your head. Also, I added a thin removable chin rest. This is what it looks like on the inside.

I don't have many progress pics of hand sewing the fur mask together but this is the way it looked before putting it onto the base. I felt I had to hand sew it to get a very small seam that wouldn't bulge and deform against the base. Kinda Silence of the Lambs vibes, right?

After almost 3 weeks of hand-sewing the mask for 2-5 hours a day, I finally finished the face and neck. This was the first time seeing it "properly" stretched over the base it was made for.

This is what it would look like "improperly" stretched over the base, but with the eyes, teeth, hair, and foam base ears in place to get an idea of how progress is going and the visual direction it's headed in.

Once the face is sewn together, baseball stitching in the ears is next. The inner ear is just black felt to give a sense of depth and the foam lobes are wrapped in more fur which is hot glued on. After the ears are in place, I added select areas of thin 1/4" foam to the interior of the fur mask. I felt this would help keep things looking rounded and plump; there wouldn't be any chance for loose fur to be wrinkled in these areas and it helped reduce visibility of seams. The entire fur mask is ready for permanent affixation to the base!

This was another novel thing I tried out in my build process: I wanted to REALLY be sure that the fur would never come unglued in a few critical points along the face; if they did, there would be no way to fix it once the fabric fusion glue dries. These are places like concave dimples, the underside of cheeks, the furled eyebrow/nose bridge, etc.. Anywhere with significant concave curves. My idea was to drill very small holes in those spots, stretch the fur mask over the base, then push sewing needles through the holes and mask to create a loop of threading. I would tie the threading off with a lot of slack in the loops like this:

I wanted not only to make permanent physical anchors holding these key spots in place, but also help dramatically speed up my working time while glueing the mask to the base. I opted for fabric fusion after weighing a few options because it was a clear permanent fabric glue with a "long" working time of ~ 30 minutes which still felt like very little time.

By "pre-stretching" the mask over the base to add these thread loop anchors, I was able to grab all of them from the back and pull them tight one action, then tie them off so the base was held still in the correct way while it dried over a full day. It added an extra few hours of prep work to try this technique but I'm really happy with how well it worked in the end.

This is what it looked like after fully applying the glue! The masking tape is wrapping around all un-sewn edges and into the interior to hold the fur tight against the base.

The mohawk is a long triangular strip of foam with 2 strips of red fur sewn together and glued to it. That mohawk was then baseball stitched into the fur head and down along the back of the neck. The baseball stitching is surprisingly loose and spread out; you can wiggle your finger through it from one side to the other but it worked really well and you'd never know.

Hands

I followed a pattern for the hand-paws I found online and I'm really pleased with how they came out. It's not perfectly the way I'd want it to look but it's good and to instantly have access to a good pattern is hard to come by. I started by creating all the claws out of stretchy black velvet/velour material, stuffing them with polyfil, then sewing them into the tops of each finger. They're inserted into a dart in the fabric then it's all sewn shut.

For the pads beneath claws, I taped and pinned the template guide into the backside of the fabric then sewed all the pads in one go with a single large sheet of velour then cut the unneeded velour away as close to the stitching as I could. This created empty pads on the fur side of the fabric, and I cut small openings into the fur which polyfil could be stuffed in and then sewn shut again. This creates large plushy pads, aka beans.

Once top and bottom halves were finished, it was time to painstakingly sew them together (very hard to feed the fabric in at webbing of fingers). then flip them inside out to see the finished result

Bodysuit

Unless you're a tailor, the most straight-forward way to get super accurate pattern of your body is to make a duct tape dummy. There are tons of videos online showing how to do this and it's a several person job. You basically wear a cheap and thin painter suit while others wrap you in 2 layers of duct tape then cut you out of it.

I built up a skin-tight masking tape layer around the dummy that I can use to draw on without worrying that I'm affecting the dummy itself (in case revisions need to be done).

Once I finished drawing my pattern onto the dummies masking tape shell, I cut it off and used the shapes as patterns for cutting the fur. There are tons of videos online explaining this process. For my first bodysuit I decided to make it plantigrade (no special padding)

Once all pieces were cut out (several days of effort) I double checked they were all there by laying them out.

I decided to sew it up in left and right halves independently which helped make the amount of material you're working with at any moment much more manageable. I added a zipper down the middle front and I followed this AMAZING deluxe zipper guide.

Feet

The feet start as a pair of cheap crocs from Walmart, which are then glued with e6000 to eva foam mats. The mats are cut out to the general footprint shape desired, an thick 1" foam is glued into the shape of toes. I followed a pattern for the first pair I made and they were WAAAYY to big. I ditched the pattern and free-handed the footprint to be as small as I could make it to still fit the shoes then re-did the foam toes in the same way as before, but smaller this time.

Once the overall shape felt good enough, I added some stiff foam around the back of the heels then masking taped up one of the feet to get a fur pattern for it.

I cut out the fur based on the pattern, sewed it into a "cover" for each foot (doing a mirrored pattern for the other foot), then glued it all down with hot glue and e6000.

Not pictured yet is the rubber threading I added to the underside of the feet. I didn't realize how insanely slippery the feet are on wet surfaces till I walked on one and nearly fell. It's no joke how slippery the feet were on smooth wet floors.
